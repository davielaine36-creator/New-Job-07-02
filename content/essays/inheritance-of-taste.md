People talk about taste as though it were a fixed possession — you have it or you don't, like perfect pitch. I think this is exactly backwards. Taste is not a gift. It is an accumulation. It is the compound interest of attention paid over years to things that were slightly better than they had to be.

## The long argument

Every judgment you make about quality is in conversation with every judgment you have made before. When you say a thing is *right*, you are not consulting a rulebook. You are consulting a private history of encounters — the first time a sentence stopped you, the room that made you go quiet, the object you kept picking up.

That history can be cultivated. You can choose what you stand next to.

## Standing next to better things

The fastest way to refine taste is proximity. Spend time with work that is genuinely excellent and your tolerance for the mediocre quietly collapses. Not through snobbery — through calibration. The eye simply learns.

This is why the environment matters so much. Surround yourself with the almost-good and your ceiling becomes the almost-good. Surround yourself with the exceptional and something in you rises, uninvited, to meet it.

---

Taste, then, is less a verdict than a practice. You build it the way you build anything worth having: slowly, deliberately, and mostly by refusing to settle. It is the one inheritance you get to write yourself.
